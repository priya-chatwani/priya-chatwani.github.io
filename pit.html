Discovering Public Interest Tech
I entered the field of computer science because of an impactful class I took senior year of high school: Human-Centered Design. It was the first time that the class had been taught, and the ten of us felt empowered and excited as we learned about innovations that authentically met people's needs.

In my college degree, however, I did not easily find an emphasis on human-centered technology. And at my Google and Twitter internships, it was equally hard to find. Instead, I felt unfulfilled and out of place as I worked on technology that didn't excite me or have a visible positive impact. And after participating in an educational and immersive "alternative spring break" trip about housing inequality in the Bay Area, I realized how destructive these tech companies are to their surrounding communities.

In my senior year, an exciting opportunity came my way - I was hired as the first Public Interest Technology Student Assistant at the Haas Center for Public Service. In this role, I researched, created, and and raised awareness about public interest tech career and volunteer opportunities. Some of my tasks included:
  • adding public interest tech placements to our post-grad fellowships. This included reaching out to numerous tech non profits and government branches and conversing with their recruiters and employees.
  • creating an open-source list of all the public interest tech organizations and contacts in collaboration with students groups like CS+Social Good.
  • starting a public interest tech careers newsletter. This involved looking through dozens of job boards for internships and entry-level positions each week.
  • hosting panels on technology's relationship to ethics and Covid-19, racial justice, and politics in collaboration with other student groups & university departments.
  • facilitating collaboration and communication about public interest tech across different labs, departments, student groups, and organizations.
  • advocating for awareness of public interest tech among mainstream computer science events, such as the annual hackathon and career fairs.
  • recording a presentation about finding a career in public interest tech.
  • creating and sharing flyers for public interest tech events.
I was lucky enough to find an entry-level, public interest tech role at Remix, where we build design and analysis tools for transit agencies and departments of transportation. The way we center the needs of city planners and urban residents brings me back to that high school Human-Centered Design course. I hope roles like mine can become more accessible and I hope we can shift the tide in how technology is used - to uplift and empower those who have long been fighting for a more just world.


Public Interest Tech Career Guide
  1. Be intentional about your job search.

    My friend and mentor, Aishwarya Vardhana, advised me to explicitly think about my values and the change I want to help bring about. She said to document the following:

    • What drives me and gives me energy?
    • What are issues I care about? What things in society are immoral, inefficient, or wrong?
    • What are solutions to these problems? What companies are working in these areas?
    • What areas or applications of technology excite me?
    • What are my must-haves at a company (i.e. at least two women of color in leadership)?
    • What are my deal breakers for a company?

  2. Learn about the Public Interest Tech landscape.

    As you begin your job search, it is helpful to know what options are available to you (see my Medium article). Some areas that fall under public interest tech include:

    • For-Profit Tech with a Social Mission (i.e. Remix, Propel)
    • Tech nonprofits (i.e. Upsolve, Raheem)
    • Civic and government tech (i.e. CityInnovate, Code for America)
    • Advocacy groups and political campaigns (i.e. ACLU Digital & Technologies, Alloy)
    • Academia and Research (i.e. Electronic Frontier Foundation, AI Now Institute @ NYU)
    • Tech activism (i.e. Silicon Valley Rising, Mijente)
    • Mitigate the harmful effects of tech, such as algorithmic bias or data privacy
    • Social impact work or corporate social responsibility at a large tech firm

  5. Build your network.

Rock The Boat
Now that you‚Äôve landed a job in tech, here are some recommendations on how to #RockTheBoat, shake it up, and create lasting change.

NOTE: Please consider how your various identities affect how you are perceived. These actions can negatively impact one‚Äôs job security, and the penalty is much higher for folks from marginalized communities.
  1. Join an identity-based group.

    ERGs have become a crucial part of change in the tech industry at large corporations. These identity-based groups allow BIPOC folks, womxn, LGBTQ+ members, and others to support and advocate for one another as a group.

    If you want to join an ERG as an ally or manager, check with the group before doing so and be aware of how much space you take up. The goal is to center and support folks who don‚Äôt always have a voice.

    If your organization does not have ERGs, advocate for them to be created! They have positive impacts on employees‚Äô sense of belonging and are good for business as well. But it doesn‚Äôt stop there. Ensure that the time and energy spent on ERGs are valued and seen as work work. Advocate for executive funding and sponsorship. Being in an ERG does not make you the diversity educator of your company. In fact, it should reflect positively in performance reviews.

  2. Ask the hard questions.

    To those in positions of privilege who are not concerned about job security:

    • Put your neck on the line when hard questions about diversity, inclusion, and equity need to be asked.
    • Yes, your heart should be pounding and your pits should be sweating as you read your question aloud.
    • If you can use statistics to back up your question.
    • Ask your question in a public forum or send it in a public chat. This not only holds members of authority accountable, as they should be held to a higher standard, but it also brings wider visibility to important problems.

  3. Push for inclusive hiring practices.

    Another good place to start is surrounding hiring practices. Work with ERGs and/or a group of like-minded employees to create a list of requests. Some items that you can request:

    • Is there a rubric for all interviews? Is that rubric shared with the candidate beforehand? Have the rubric and interview questions been reviewed by ERGs and others at the company? Without a rubric, it is too easy for interviewers to fall back on their biases and go with whoever they deem ‚Äúlikeable‚Äù, aka whoever is the most like them.
    • Is there at least one woman or non-binary person and at least one BIPOC employee in every interview?
    • How a candidate speaks to and interacts with folks from underrepresented groups is a striking reflection of their character.
    • Can the hiring team openly share the demographic breakdowns of all job applicants? How about the demographic breakdowns of applicant drop off at various stages in the pipeline?
    • Before extending an offer to an applicant, interview at least one woman or non-binary person and at least one BIPOC person.
    • How can employees be involved in the interview process?
    • Where is the position being advertised? Is it being shared with identity-based groups, conferences, community forums, and bootcamps like Include.io, AfroTech, and Techqueria?
    • Can department-specific demographic breakdowns of all employees be tracked and openly shared?

  4. Get involved in tech activist groups!

    The Tech Workers Coalition is a great way to work in solidarity with other tech workers who are pressuring the industry from within and holding it accountable. Another group to check out is Collective Action in Tech, which documents collective actions and allows workers to tell their stories, share resources, and theorize the tech workers movement together.

  5. Who are your service workers???

    Almost every company employs service workers, and tech often goes the extra mile by hiring chefs, cooks, servers, hashers, security guards, janitors, and maybe even shuttle bus drivers. Tech needs to do better in sharing their wealth with ALL employees.

    According to advocacy group Silicon Valley Rising, ‚ÄúIn the past two decades, the number of Silicon Valley jobs in subcontracted industries has grown three times as fast as overall Silicon Valley employment,‚Äù yet many of these service workers are paid poorly and not given basic benefits.

  6. Where does your company invest and spend its $$?

    Encourage your company to purchase perks, gifts, and office supplies/decor from sustainable, local, Black-owned, BIPOC-owned, womxn-owned, and worker cooperative businesses. Say no to Amazon, and check out this guide to Buying Black (created by Sista Circle: Black Women in Tech).
